Follow the Honey

Jun 5th, 2014

A few weeks ago I was dragged somewhat skeptically to a honey store. Yep, that’s right—a honey store. A store, that’s dedicated to honey. I know. Strange as it may sound, it’s actually a thing. But after my (thus far only) trip, I’m a convert.

The storefront is very cozy, nestled within the basement of a building in Harvard Square. The inside is all amber and, to the surprise of absolutely nobody, decorated with various bee-related paraphernalia—Everything from cards with bees to beeswax candles. Why couldn’t I have known about this place back when we were doing caneles? The first attempts with copper molds all but required beeswax; hell, I still prefer that to cooking spray. But I digress.

The workers were all super happy and friendly. I wonder if it was the general almost-hippie peaceful vibe of the interior, but they almost seemed too happy. As in I was almost tempted to ask for the, ahem, “special” honey, so to speak. In any case, they were all perfectly pleasant and helpful and eager to offer samples from the honey bar. Did I mention they have a honey bar? It’s absolutely bizarre. The first question I was asked was “what flavors do you like in your honey?” I have no idea, are you kidding me? I’ve only had the kind in the bear bottles.

I ended up trying a few varieties ranging from the savory and spicy to the sweet and fruity. And it’s like coffee—Once you have the real stuff, you don’t want to go back unless you have no other choice. For my part, I walked out with nearly 3 digits’ worth of merch.

The most interesting is probably the Hawaiian honey, which is from bees gathering from flowers off the side of volcanos in Hawaii. If that sounds exotic, don’t worry—It’s got a correspondingly exotic price tag, about 30-some dollars for 8 oz (226g). I bought two jars: one was Kiawe honey infused separately with lilikoi flowers, and one uninfused Macadamia honey. The former actually came in semi-solid form (common for raw honey) and packed a sharp passion fruit flavor. The latter was a more “typical” liquid texture, and rosey gold with a mild nutty flavor.

In addition to these, I also walked out with two jars of honey gathered from Oaxaca, Mexico. Oaxaca’s a great region for food agriculture—Some pretty good coffee comes out of there as well. This honey was very savory, almost spicey, and a dark maple-syrupy color. It was also very reasonably priced, something like $12-13 for I would guess 12oz out of the keg. Oh, did I mention there were kegs? Honey, on tap. Whodaknownit?

If it isn’t obvious by now, I’d recommend anyone in the area to stop by at least once, regardless of inherent interest in bees and honey. If nothing else there are very nice people giving out free samples, and who knows? Maybe just a few tastes will be enough.

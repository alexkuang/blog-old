It’s that time of the year again, folks. That’s right. I’m playing with another Keller recipe. This time, it started when I saw a few slices of pork belly over at HMart, and for some reason I immediately thought of Keller’s pork belly confit recipe in Ad Hoc At Home so I went and bought it. Well, as it turns out I don’t have nearly enough duck fat or lard to do a proper confit, so I decided to follow his braised pork belly recipe instead.

Keller does give quantities to his ingredients, but… I will not be providing them here. It is not because of some stupid Intellectual Property thing (recipes fortunately are pretty much free game), but I respect him both as a chef and as a cookbook writer, and I think you should buy his book… Here is an amazon link. (See, no affiliate code, so Amazon isn’t paying me for your clicks. Just saying.)

Anyhow, let’s get started…

Ingredients

  • Basil
  • Thyme
  • Parsley
  • Rosemary
  • Honey
  • Bay leaves
  • Salt
  • Peppercorns
  • Garlic
  • Pork Belly, with skin removed
  • Beef Stock

Methodology

  1. Throw everything but the beef stock and the pork belly into a big pot of water, big enough to hold everything at once. Bring the mixture to a boil, and boil for one minute. Afterwards, let it cool down and chill in the fridge. This brine can be kept for up to 3 days in the fridge after initial preparation.
  2. Put the pork into the brine. Let it rest in the fridge for 10 hours; Keller recommends no more, or the pork will get over-salty.
  3. Pre-heat your oven to 320 degrees F. While it’s warming up, put the pork into an oven-safe saute pan fat-side down <<<<<<< HEAD and cook it on medium-low heat, until the fat is a beautiful golden brown and nicely rendered, about 15 - 20 minutes. ======= and cook it on medium-low heat, until the fat is a beautiful golden brown and nicely rendered, about 15 – 20 minutes. >>>>>>> 3f751f29d79be717da3f49498942848728c0ff04 Be sure to get rid of the excess fat that gets rendered out.
  4. Put enough of the beef stock into the pan to come about ½ up the side of the pork, then cover the pan with a parchment lid (basically a circular sheet of parchment with a hole in the middle, big enough to cover your pan) and put it into the oven.. Let the pork cook until tender, about 2.5 hours.
  5. After it’s done cooking take it out to let it cool down, then put the pork and the liquid into a tray, and cover with plastic wrap. Place a smaller tray on top, and weigh it down with something heavy like a can or a bottle; I used a jar of honey and citron:
    The purpose of this is to compress the layers of the pork and give it a better texture at the end. I personally tested this by leaving one of my pieces of pork un-compressed, and it does make a noticeable difference. Let this rest in the fridge like this for 12 hours.
  6. The pork can be stored like this in its liquid for 3 days. To serve though, pre-heat your oven to 400 degrees fahrenheit. Remove any fat that may have solidified in your braising liquid, then put the pork fat side down again into the pan, with enough liquid to come up ¼ inch of the side of the meat. Bring the stock to a simmer and baste it with the liquid.
  7. Transfer the meat to the oven and cook, basting a few times, for about 15 minutes. Then flip the meat over so it’s fat side up, and cook for another 5 minutes, keeping up the continual basting so the meat becomes glazed and a nice rich brown color.
  8. Reduce the remaining braising liquid in a pan to a thicker sauce-like consistency, and serve it with the belly. I decided to cut mine into little chunks, and here is the result:

Notes

  • The first and foremost note here is that pork belly is delicious, but EXTREMELY rich. In fact, there is nearly a 1:1 lean meat:fat ratio. As such… It is HIGHLY NOT RECOMMENDED to eat the amount shown in the picture in one sitting. The plate that was in the picture was shared between myself and two others, and lasted roughly 2.5 meals served with rice and another side dish.
  • Yes, it was delicious.
